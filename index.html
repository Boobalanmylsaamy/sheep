History of Sheep

The exact line of descent from wild ancestors to domestic sheep is unclear.The most common hypothesis states that Ovis aries is descended from the Asiatic species of mouflon; the European mouflon (Ovis aries musimon) is a direct descendant of this population.Sheep were among the first animals to be domesticated by humankind (although the domestication of dogs probably took place 10 to 20 thousand years earlier); the domestication date is estimated to fall between 11,000 and 9,000 B.C in Mesopotamia and possibly around 7,000 B.C. in Mehrgarh in the Indus Valley.

Sheep husbandry spread quickly in Europe.     • What Do Sheep Eat?

    Sheep are grazers which means they wander pastures and enjoy eating the entirety of plants like grass, clover, forbs, and legumes. During winter, when grazing isn't an option, sheep are fed by their farmers stored hay, barley, corn, and other grains. However, sheep prefer to eat leafy plants over fibrous plant parts and foods. Therefore, sheep farmers prefer to avoid feeding grains and highly fibrous plants like hay and alfalfa. However, alfalfa hay has a high protein content and may be used secondarily for sheep.


    Sheep prefer leafy greens over fibrous parts of plants and other plant products such as grains. Therefore, it is best to allow sheep to obtain most of their energy from grazing or wandering pastureland and eating grasses and leafy clovers, chicory, legumes, and shrubs. When sheep are harvested for their meat, they are often raised and fed through grazing. However, if sheep are being raised for milk or pregnant, they have supplemented foods high in protein, such as grains and hays. These foods are less available to wild sheep and can cause health problems in excess. Sheep may also be supplemented with minerals like salts through salt blocks.
